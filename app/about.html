How TCU Career Compass Works

This page helps viewers understand what the app is showing, how the datasets are organized, and how to interpret the map, city cards, and quick snapshot insights.

Purpose

TCU Career Compass is a city exploration tool designed to help viewers compare where different career paths may be strongest after graduation. Instead of showing one generic job market, the app organizes city data by major and then by career path so users can explore options that align with their field of study.

How To Use It

1. Choose A Major

Start with the major dropdown. Once a major is selected, the map limits itself to locations connected to that major’s career paths.

2. Choose A Career Path

Career path buttons appear based on the selected major. Clicking one loads that specific dataset and updates the city list, insights, and map to match it.

3. Explore The Results

Users can compare top cities, read quick snapshot summaries, and click map markers to open location popups with occupation and wage details.

What The Data Means

Metro

The metro field is the full metropolitan statistical area used as the location label throughout the app.

Annual Mean Wage

This is the average yearly wage for the related occupation in that metro area.

Employment

Employment shows how many people work in that occupation within the metro area.

Location Quotient

Location quotient compares how concentrated an occupation is in a metro relative to the national average. Higher values suggest stronger specialization.

Regional Price Parity (RPP)

RPP is a 2024 BEA cost-of-living index (100 = national average). States with RPP > 100 are more expensive; states with RPP < 100 are less expensive. Used to adjust wages for fair comparison across regions.

Adjusted Annual Mean Wage

This is the annual mean wage adjusted for regional cost-of-living differences using RPP. It shows what the salary is worth in purchasing power relative to the national average, enabling fair salary comparisons across different cost-of-living areas.

Occupation

Each career path can be connected to a BLS-style occupation label, such as Management Analysts or Data Scientists.

How The Quick Snapshot Works

Highest Salary

This card identifies the city with the largest adjusted_annual_mean_wage in the selected career path. The adjusted wage accounts for regional cost-of-living differences, enabling fair salary comparison across metros.

Highest Location Quotient

This card identifies the city with the largest location_quotient, meaning the strongest occupation concentration.

Best Adjusted Wage

This card identifies the city with the largest adjusted_annual_mean_wage, which represents the strongest financial opportunity after accounting for regional cost-of-living. This is used to rank the city list and highlight top locations on the map.

How Ranking & Comparison Work

Cities are ranked by adjusted_annual_mean_wage in descending order. This cost-of-living-adjusted salary is the primary metric used to identify top opportunities because it reflects real earning power across different regional markets. The app uses this metric to rank the city list, identify the top-earning location for each occupation, and color map markers.

How The Map Works

The map places each city using its latitude and longitude. Marker colors reflect the percentile rank of that metro's adjusted wage among all metros in the dataset: darker purple indicates higher-earning locations (top 10%, 20%, 30%), while lighter purple indicates lower-earning ones. Standout brighter markers identify the highest-earning location for each occupation in the current map view. Clicking a marker opens a popup with the metro name, occupation, wages, employment, location quotient, and RPP details.

How The Files Are Organized

The datasets are grouped by major inside the data folder. Each major has its own folder, and each career path points to one JSON file. This keeps the project scalable and makes it easier to swap in updated datasets later.

Data Sources

U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)

Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) program provides the foundation data: annual mean wages, employment counts by occupation and metro area, and labor market demand data.

U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA)

2024 Regional Price Parities (RPP) provide state-level cost-of-living indices that adjust national wage data for regional price differences, enabling fair salary comparisons across metros with different living costs.

U.S. Census Bureau

Defines metropolitan statistical areas (MSAs) used throughout the app as geographic boundaries for metro-level data aggregation and analysis.

Custom Model

Career mapping connects BLS occupations to TCU major and career path categories, and affordability-adjusted metrics combine cost-of-living data with wage and employment information to provide cost-aware career insights.
