Research Question

How do cost-of-living affordability and career opportunities vary across US cities for different business majors?

This research question addresses the practical challenge faced by undergraduate business students at TCU: understanding which cities offer both strong career prospects and manageable living costs for their specific major and career path.

Research Approach

This project combines quantitative data analysis with interactive visualization. It uses government-sourced datasets (wages, employment, cost indices) to compare affordability across U.S. metropolitan areas, while allowing users to explore patterns and derive personalized insights based on their situation.

Data Collection Methods

U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) - OEWS Data

Source: Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) program

What was collected: Annual mean wages, employment counts, and location quotient data for occupations by metropolitan statistical area (MSA). Data was aggregated at the MSA level to enable city-level comparisons.

How it was collected: Downloaded via the BLS Data Tools portal and public datasets, with API queries structured to retrieve specific occupation and geography combinations relevant to business career paths.

U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) - Regional Price Parities

Source: Regional Price Parities (RPP) index, 2024

What was collected: State-level cost-of-living indices (base year 100 = national average) to adjust wages for regional affordability differences.

Why Regional Price Parities (RPP): Rather than tracking housing/rent separately (which varies by buy vs. rent, bedrooms, and neighborhood), RPP captures comprehensive cost-of-living: housing, transportation, food, utilities, and services. This holistic approach provides a truer picture of purchasing power across regions.

How it was collected: 2024 RPP data accessed through BEA's official publications and aggregated by state to enable cost-of-living adjustments at the metro level.

Career Path Mapping

Source: TCU Neeley School of Business major and career path definitions

What was collected: Mapping of TCU business majors (Finance, Accounting, Marketing, BIS, Supply Chain, Management, Innovation & Entrepreneurship) to relevant BLS occupations and career specializations.

How it was collected: To connect data to college majors, each major was mapped to popular entry-level occupations using standardized BLS occupation categories. This required interpretation and simplification, as no direct dataset exists that links majors to careers in a one-to-one format. The process involved researching the top entry-level jobs for each major, then matching them to the closest BLS occupation codes. This manual mapping step was critical for ensuring academic relevance while maintaining data integrity.

Data Management & Preparation

Data Cleaning

Raw BLS data was cleaned to remove duplicate records, fill missing values using forward/backward fill methods where appropriate, and standardize geographic identifiers. Invalid or incomplete occupation-metro combinations were excluded.

Data Organization

Each career path is mapped to a specific BLS occupation. For example, "Financial Analysis" maps to "Financial Analysts," and "Credit & Lending" maps to "Credit Analysts." Each JSON file contains that occupation's wage and employment data across all metro areas in the dataset.

Cost-of-Living Adjustment Formula

To make wages comparable across cities, a calculated field called adjusted_annual_mean_wage was created using the formula:

adjusted_annual_mean_wage = annual_mean_wage ÷ (RPP ÷ 100)

This transformation normalizes salaries to a national baseline by accounting for regional cost-of-living differences. As a result, the adjusted wage reflects purchasing power rather than nominal income, enabling fair comparison across high-cost and low-cost metros.

Data Formatting & Storage

The data was cleaned and structured into JSON files organized by major and career pathway. Each dataset includes metropolitan area names, occupation titles, wage data, and adjusted wage values. This structure allows the application to load and display relevant data based on user input. JSON format was chosen because it keeps datasets easy to manage and enables direct client-side consumption without requiring backend infrastructure.

Data Analysis Methods

Descriptive Statistics

For each career path and metro combination, percentile ranks of adjusted wages are calculated. These percentile ranks determine the marker colors on the map (darker purple for higher-earning locations), providing a visual indication of wage distributions across regions.

Location Quotient (LQ) Analysis

Location quotient identifies metros where specific occupations are disproportionately concentrated. LQ values > 1.0 indicate above-average occupation specialization, highlighting metros where career paths are particularly strong.

Ranking & Comparative Analysis

For each career path, metros are ranked by adjusted annual mean wage in descending order. This ranking identifies top earning opportunities while accounting for regional cost differences, enabling fair cross-metro comparison.

Interactive Visualization & Exploratory Analysis

Data is visualized on an interactive map with color-coded markers (darker = higher-earning locations). Users can filter by major/career path and explore trade-offs between salary, employment, and cost of living in real time.

Methodological Choices & Project Scope

MSA-Level Geographic Aggregation: Analysis is conducted at the metropolitan statistical area (MSA) level rather than individual city level. This follows BLS reporting standards and provides more stable employment and wage data while covering most major US labor markets.

Cost-of-Living Adjustment: The decision to adjust wages using Regional Price Parities was critical to answering the research question. Without adjustment, high-wage cities in expensive metros would be overvalued, leading to misleading recommendations. RPP adjustment enables true purchasing power comparison.

2024 Data Baseline: The project uses 2024 data to ensure relevance for current students making career and location decisions. While this limits historical trend analysis, it maximizes applicability to the target audience.

Focus on Seven Business Majors: The scope was intentionally limited to TCU's business school majors rather than all 200+ BLS occupations. This manageable scope enables meaningful career-path-specific insights while keeping the tool approachable for users.

Occupation-to-Major Mapping: BLS occupations were manually mapped to TCU career paths based on curriculum alignment and industry practice. This introduces an element of human judgment but ensures academic relevance to the target audience.

Metrics Selection: The decision to prioritize adjusted annual mean wage as the ranking metric (over median wage or location quotient alone) reflects the assumption that earning potential is a primary decision factor. Alternative weighting schemes could be supported in future versions.

Application Development & Technologies

Frontend Stack

The application was built using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, with the Leaflet library used to create an interactive map interface. Vanilla JavaScript (no framework) was chosen for performance and simplicity. Users can select a major, and the application dynamically updates to display cities, job roles, and wage data associated with that selection.

Data Processing & Backend

Data cleaning, aggregation, and statistical analysis were performed in Python using libraries: pandas (data manipulation), numpy (numerical operations), and json (data export). JavaScript is used to load and filter datasets, apply calculations, and update the UI in real time.

Data Storage & Distribution

All datasets are stored in JSON format, organized by major and career pathway. This keeps them easy to manage and enables static file serving without backend infrastructure, reducing deployment complexity and improving performance.

Limitations & Future Directions

Data Recency: BLS data reflects labor market conditions at a single point in time (2024). Future versions should incorporate trend data and quarterly updates to track market evolution.

Geographic Coverage: Analysis is limited to major MSAs covered by BLS OEWS. Smaller metros and rural markets are not represented. Expanding data coverage could provide additional options for users.

Occupation-to-Major Mapping: The manual mapping of BLS occupations to TCU majors introduces subjectivity. A formal curriculum-to-occupation validation process with academic advisors could refine this mapping.

Additional Factors Not Considered: The tool prioritizes salary and employment but does not factor in other quality-of-life metrics (cultural amenities, climate, proximity to family). Future versions could integrate additional datasets.

User Behavior Tracking: Currently, the app does not log which cities or career paths users explore most. Adding anonymized analytics could reveal which combinations are most of interest and guide future research.

Ethical Considerations

Data Transparency: All data sources are publicly documented and directly linked. Users can verify underlying statistics and understand how conclusions were derived.

Avoiding Bias in Recommendations: The tool presents data without directing users toward "optimal" choices. It aims to inform rather than prescribe, recognizing that career decisions involve personal values beyond salary and employment rates.

Occupation Privacy: Data is aggregated at the occupation level; no individual-level employment data is disclosed, protecting worker privacy while maintaining analytical utility.
