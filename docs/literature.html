What We Know

Housing and Overall Affordability: Housing costs vary dramatically by region due to supply constraints and agglomeration effects (Glaeser and Nathanson, 2017). But housing is just one piece. Food, transportation, utilities, and services also vary regionally. The Bureau of Economic Analysis tracks Regional Price Parities (RPP), measuring overall cost-of-living differences across regions. A $100k salary in San Francisco isn't the same as $100k in Dallas.

Geographic Wage Disparities: Wages vary substantially by region due to agglomeration effects, human capital concentration, and local labor market conditions (Autor and Dorn, 2013). Cities like San Francisco (technology), New York (finance), and Los Angeles (entertainment) show strong industry clustering that offers career benefits but also drives up living costs. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics and NACE document that career location decisions significantly impact earning potential.

Why Industry Clustering Happens: Research on agglomeration explains why certain regions concentrate specific industries (Ellison, Glaeser, and Kerr, 2010). Networking, knowledge spillovers, and labor market depth create self-reinforcing clusters. The tradeoff between agglomeration benefits and the higher costs of living in these clusters has received less study for entry-level workers.

What We Don't Know: Research Gaps

Affordability for Entry-Level Workers: While housing affordability research is extensive, there is limited systematic research on overall affordability (not just housing) for entry-level workers making initial career location decisions. Most research focuses either on aggregate regional statistics or on high-cost coastal metros. The comparative affordability landscape for mid-sized cities and the trade-offs between earning potential and cost of living for recent graduates remain understudied.

Business Major Geographic Patterns: Business schools track where their graduates work, but they don't usually compare affordability across different cities for different majors. So if you're a Finance grad trying to decide between New York and Dallas, or a Marketing grad looking at Atlanta versus San Francisco, you'd have to piece that information together yourself. This project puts all of that in one place.

Interactive Data Visualization in Academic Research: Most research presents findings through tables and charts. Interactive maps and tools that let you filter by major or city are limited in published academic work. This project aims to change that by making data explorable instead of just readable.

How This Project Fits Into Existing Research

Addressing Research Gaps: This project takes existing research about housing, cost of living, and job markets and puts it together in one place. Instead of reading academic papers, you can actually use this tool to explore where you might move after graduation.

Methodological Innovation: The data comes from official sources like the Bureau of Labor Statistics and cost-of-living indices. The difference is how it's presented. Instead of publishing a research paper, this creates a tool you can actually play with and explore.

Disciplinary Contributions: This project pulls from several different fields: economics (wages and cost of living), urban studies (how cities differ), higher education (what happens to graduates), and design (building tools people want to use). By showing how cost-of-living matters for graduate decisions, it opens up new questions for future research.

Conclusion: The Research Gap This Project Addresses

Existing research has thoroughly documented housing affordability challenges, geographic wage disparities, and graduate employment outcomes. However, there is a significant gap in integrated, accessible analysis that combines cost-of-living adjusted wages, employment concentration, and career-path-specific geographic patterns to support entry-level worker decision-making.

This project addresses that gap by creating an interactive tool. Instead of reading research papers or trying to piece together job data with cost-of-living info yourself, you can actually explore where to live for your major and career path, with real numbers about both opportunities and affordability. By combining labor market data, cost-of-living indices, and interactive visualization, it translates academic research into something actionable for college students and recent graduates. In doing so, it contributes to the broader conversation about geographic inequality, career decision-making, and the practical challenges facing young professionals in the U.S. labor market.
