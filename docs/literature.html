What We Know: Cost Variation, Career Outcomes, and Geographic Disparities

Housing Cost Burden and Regional Variation: Research documents that housing affordability is a critical challenge in the United States. The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development defines housing affordability as spending no more than 30% of household income on housing costs. Studies by the Joint Center for Housing Studies (JCHS) at Harvard University show that housing costs vary dramatically by region—from San Francisco and New York to more affordable metros—due to supply constraints, zoning policies, and agglomeration effects (Glaeser and Nathanson, 2017).

Graduate Employment and Wage Geography: Extensive research documents employment outcomes for college graduates. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes employment and earnings data by occupation and region, while organizations like NACE document that career location decisions significantly impact earning potential. Labor economics research shows that wages vary substantially by geographic region, driven by agglomeration effects, human capital concentration, and local labor market conditions (Bound and Holzer, 2000). More recent work examines how geographic concentration of different job types affects regional inequality (Autor and Dorn, 2013).

Agglomeration and Cost-of-Living Tradeoffs: Research on agglomeration economies explains why certain regions concentrate specific industries and occupations. Cities like San Francisco (technology), New York (finance and media), and Los Angeles (entertainment) demonstrate strong clustering effects that offer networking and career advancement benefits. However, these clusters also drive up living costs. Research by the Pew Research Center shows that housing and cost-of-living concerns influence where recent graduates choose to live. The tradeoff between agglomeration benefits and cost of living has received less systematic empirical study, particularly for entry-level workers comparing earning potential against actual affordability.

What We Know: Cost-of-Living Adjustment and Regional Price Parities

Purchasing Power Parity Research: Just comparing salaries across cities doesn't tell the full story. A $100k job in San Francisco isn't the same as a $100k job in Dallas because everything costs more in San Francisco. The Bureau of Economic Analysis uses an index called Regional Price Parities (RPP) to measure regional price differences. It includes housing, food, transportation, utilities, and services. Research shows that these cost-of-living adjustments matter a lot for how much money you actually have left to spend.

Rent vs. Comprehensive Measures: Most affordability studies focus only on housing costs. But housing is just one piece of the puzzle. Transportation, food, and utilities also matter. Research shows that some cities have cheap rent but expensive transportation or food. However, when people study graduate affordability, they usually just look at housing, not the bigger picture.

Policy and Planning Applications: Policymakers and planners use cost-of-living data for analysis, but not many resources exist to help individual graduates make decisions. Most research looks at big-picture regional inequality or focuses only on expensive cities like New York and San Francisco. There's a gap between research and practical tools that let students compare different cities for their specific career path.

What We Know: Major-Specific Career Outcomes

Business Program Outcomes: Business schools track where their graduates end up and what they earn. Research by GMAC shows that different business majors have different job markets. Finance people tend to cluster in New York and Chicago. Marketing grads spread out more. But nobody combines this job data with cost-of-living information to show which cities are actually affordable for which majors.

Occupational Concentration: Labor market research demonstrates that business graduates pursue occupations that vary significantly by region. For example, finance graduates concentrate in New York, Chicago, and San Francisco, while marketing graduates have more distributed employment patterns. Technology and data analytics roles cluster in tech hubs. The career-path-specific geographic distribution is well-documented, but affordability implications receive limited attention.

What We Don't Know: Research Gaps

Affordability for Entry-Level Workers: While housing affordability research is extensive, there is limited systematic research on overall affordability (not just housing) for entry-level workers making initial career location decisions. Most research focuses either on aggregate regional statistics or on high-cost coastal metros. The comparative affordability landscape for mid-sized cities and the trade-offs between earning potential and cost of living for recent graduates remain understudied.

Integrated Decision-Making Tools: Researchers know about job markets, they know about cost of living, and they know where jobs concentrate. But nobody puts all three together in one place, especially not in a tool students can actually use. Interactive tools that let you explore these tradeoffs are basically nonexistent.

Business Major Geographic Patterns: Business schools know where their graduates work. But there's no systematic research comparing how affordable different cities are for different career paths. How does Dallas compare to New York for a Finance grad? What about Atlanta versus San Francisco for a Marketing grad? That's the gap this project fills.

Interactive Data Visualization in Academic Research: Most research just publishes tables and charts. Interactive maps and tools that let you filter by major or city? Those barely exist in academic work. This project is trying to change that by making data explorable instead of just readable.

How This Project Fits Into Existing Research

Addressing Research Gaps: This project takes existing research about housing, cost of living, and job markets and puts it together in one place. Instead of reading academic papers, you can actually use this tool to explore where you might move after graduation.

Methodological Innovation: The data comes from official sources like the Bureau of Labor Statistics and cost-of-living indices. The difference is how it's presented. Instead of publishing a research paper, this creates a tool you can actually play with and explore. That's the innovation.

Disciplinary Contributions: This project pulls from several different fields: economics (wages and cost of living), urban studies (how cities differ), higher education (what happens to graduates), and design (building tools people want to use). By showing how cost-of-living matters for graduate decisions, it opens up new questions for future research.

Conclusion: The Research Gap This Project Addresses

Existing research has thoroughly documented housing affordability challenges, geographic wage disparities, and graduate employment outcomes. However, there is a significant gap in integrated, accessible analysis that combines cost-of-living adjusted wages, employment concentration, and career-path-specific geographic patterns to support entry-level worker decision-making.

This project addresses that gap by creating an interactive tool that applies well-established research methods to answer a practical question: Where should I live for my major, considering both career opportunities and affordability? By combining labor market data, cost-of-living indices, and interactive visualization, the project translates academic research into actionable information for college students and recent graduates. In doing so, it contributes to the broader conversation about geographic inequality, career decision-making, and the practical challenges facing young professionals in the U.S. labor market.
