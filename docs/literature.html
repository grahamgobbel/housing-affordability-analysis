What We Know: Housing Affordability Research

Housing Cost Burden: A substantial body of research documents that housing affordability is a critical challenge in the United States. The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development defines housing affordability as spending no more than 30% of household income on housing costs. By this standard, millions of Americans face housing cost burdens (HUD, 2024). Research by the Joint Center for Housing Studies (JCHS) at Harvard University has extensively documented regional variations in housing costs and their impact on household financial stability.

Regional Variation: Studies consistently show that housing costs vary dramatically by geographic region. High-cost metros like San Francisco, New York, and Los Angeles have been the focus of significant research attention. Glaeser and Nathanson (2017) examine why some cities have experienced explosive housing price growth while others remain relatively affordable. Their research demonstrates that housing supply constraints, zoning policies, and agglomeration effects create persistent geographic disparities.

Impact on Graduates: Recent research by the Pew Research Center and the National Association of Colleges and Employers (NACE) examines how housing costs affect entry-level worker location decisions. Studies show that housing affordability concerns influence where recent graduates choose to live, with many being priced out of traditional job centers. However, most existing research focuses on housing costs in isolation, without systematically comparing them to local earning potential.

What We Know: Career Outcomes and Geographic Mobility

College Graduate Employment: Extensive research documents employment outcomes for college graduates. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics regularly publishes employment and earnings data by occupation and region. Research by organizations like NACE shows that career location decisions significantly impact earning potential and career trajectory. Graduates in STEM fields, for example, tend to have higher geographic mobility and more location flexibility than those in other fields.

Geographic Wage Disparities: Labor economics research has long documented that wages vary substantially by geographic region and occupation. Bound and Holzer (2000) study geographic wage disparities and the factors that create and maintain them, including agglomeration effects, human capital concentration, and local labor market conditions. More recent work by Autor and Dorn (2013) examines how geographic concentration of different types of jobs affects regional inequality and worker outcomes.

Agglomeration Effects: A rich literature on agglomeration economies explains why certain regions concentrate specific industries and occupations. Cities like San Francisco (technology), New York (finance and media), and Los Angeles (entertainment) demonstrate strong clustering effects. Research suggests that while these clusters offer networking and career advancement benefits, they also drive up living costs. The tradeoff between agglomeration benefits and cost of living has received less systematic empirical study.

What We Know: Cost-of-Living Adjustment and Regional Price Parities

Purchasing Power Parity Research: Economists have long recognized that nominal income comparisons across regions can be misleading without adjusting for cost-of-living differences. The Bureau of Economic Analysis's Regional Price Parities (RPP) index provides a comprehensive measure of regional price levels, accounting for housing, food, transportation, utilities, and other goods and services. Research using RPP data demonstrates that regional cost-of-living differences significantly affect real purchasing power and financial well-being.

Rent vs. Comprehensive Measures: While housing costs receive substantial research attention, there is growing recognition that housing alone does not capture total affordability. Research by the Council of Economic Advisors and work using comprehensive price indices show that regions differ not just in housing costs but in the cost of living more broadly. However, most affordability research for college graduates still focuses narrowly on housing or relies on single-metric approaches.

Policy and Planning Applications: Regional economists and urban planners increasingly use cost-of-living adjusted metrics for policy analysis. However, the application of these metrics to entry-level worker decision-making remains limited in the academic literature. Most research either focuses on aggregate regional inequality or specific high-cost metros, rather than providing accessible, comparative analysis for individual decision-makers.

What We Know: Major-Specific Career Outcomes

Business Program Outcomes: Business schools have extensively studied employment outcomes for graduates by major. Research by the Graduate Management Admission Council (GMAC) and industry associations documents that different business specializations (Finance, Marketing, Business Information Systems, etc.) have distinct labor markets, geographic concentrations, and earning trajectories. However, this research typically does not integrate cost-of-living data or provide geographic affordability comparisons.

Occupational Concentration: Labor market research demonstrates that business graduates pursue occupations that vary significantly by region. For example, finance graduates concentrate in New York, Chicago, and San Francisco, while marketing graduates have more distributed employment patterns. Technology and data analytics roles cluster in tech hubs. The career-path-specific geographic distribution is well-documented, but affordability implications receive limited attention.

What We Don't Know: Research Gaps

Affordability for Entry-Level Workers: While housing affordability research is extensive, there is limited systematic research on overall affordability (not just housing) for entry-level workers making initial career location decisions. Most research focuses either on aggregate regional statistics or on high-cost coastal metros. The comparative affordability landscape for mid-sized cities and the trade-offs between earning potential and cost of living for recent graduates remain understudied.

Integrated Decision-Making Tools: Few studies combine labor market data, cost-of-living indices, and employment concentration measures into a single, accessible framework for career decision-making. While researchers understand each component individually, the integrated analysis of wage potential, job concentration, and affordability for specific college majors is rare. Interactive, data-driven tools that visualize these relationships are even more limited in the academic literature.

Business Major Geographic Patterns: While business schools track employment outcomes, there is limited comparative research on how affordability varies across different business career paths by metropolitan area. The intersection of major selection, career path specialization, geographic location, and affordability has not been systematically studied. This project addresses this gap by providing integrated analysis of these relationships.

Interactive Data Visualization in Academic Research: While data visualization is increasingly important in research communication, the use of interactive mapping and filtering tools to support graduate career decision-making is limited. Most affordability research is presented through static tables and charts. Interactive tools that allow users to explore trade-offs and personalize analysis remain rare in published academic work.

How This Project Fits Into Existing Research

Addressing Research Gaps: This project builds on the existing literature by integrating multiple research traditions. It combines housing and cost-of-living research with labor market analysis and makes them accessible to the target audience: college students and recent graduates. By systematically comparing wage potential, employment concentration, and affordability for specific business majors across multiple metropolitan areas, the project addresses gaps in existing research.

Methodological Innovation: The project applies established research methods (using BLS occupational data, Regional Price Parities, and labor market analysis) in a novel way. Rather than presenting findings in academic papers or policy reports, it packages them into an interactive tool designed for end-user decision-making. This approach bridges the gap between academic research and practical application.

Disciplinary Contributions: The project contributes to multiple disciplines: labor economics (geographic wage analysis), urban economics (cost-of-living comparison), higher education research (graduate career outcomes), and human-computer interaction (decision support tools). By demonstrating how cost-of-living adjusted affordability analysis can inform career decisions, it suggests new directions for research in graduate employment outcomes and career planning.

Conclusion: The Research Gap This Project Addresses

Existing research has thoroughly documented housing affordability challenges, geographic wage disparities, and graduate employment outcomes. However, there is a significant gap in integrated, accessible analysis that combines cost-of-living adjusted wages, employment concentration, and career-path-specific geographic patterns to support entry-level worker decision-making.

This project addresses that gap by creating an interactive tool that applies well-established research methods to answer a practical question: Where should I live for my major, considering both career opportunities and affordability? By combining labor market data, cost-of-living indices, and interactive visualization, the project translates academic research into actionable information for college students and recent graduates. In doing so, it contributes to the broader conversation about geographic inequality, career decision-making, and the practical challenges facing young professionals in the U.S. labor market.
