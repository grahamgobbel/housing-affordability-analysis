Analysis & Findings

This section walks through what was discovered through the analysis. By examining salary patterns, cost-of-living adjustments, and geographic variations, the research reveals important insights about career outcomes and affordability for business graduates across US cities.

Key Finding: Higher Salaries ≠ Better Affordability

One of the most noticeable findings is that higher salaries do not always translate to better affordability. Cities such as New York, San Francisco, and Los Angeles consistently show high annual mean wages across multiple occupations. However, once those wages are adjusted using Regional Price Parities, their relative advantage decreases significantly.

What This Means: While these major metropolitan areas offer strong earning potential, the higher cost of living reduces how far that income actually goes. A $120,000 salary in San Francisco may have less purchasing power than a $90,000 salary in Dallas when regional price differences are factored in. This underscores the critical importance of cost-of-living adjustment in career location decisions.

Finding: Mid-Sized Metros Show Stronger Affordability

Several mid-sized metropolitan areas show stronger results after cost-of-living adjustment. Cities like Dallas-Fort Worth, Atlanta, and Charlotte tend to rank higher in terms of adjusted wages, even when their nominal salaries are lower than coastal tech hubs.

What This Means: These locations provide a better balance between income and cost of living, making them more financially sustainable options for recent graduates. A graduate earning an adjusted wage of $75,000 in Charlotte may have better financial outcomes than someone earning a nominal $95,000 in New York, because their income stretches further in the local economy.

Career Implications: This finding suggests that career location decisions should extend beyond the traditional high-paying markets. Mid-sized cities often offer strong job markets, lower living costs, and better work-life balance, making them attractive alternatives for recent graduates prioritizing affordability and financial stability.

Finding: Career Path Differences Within Majors

A significant finding is the variation in affordability outcomes across different career paths within the same major. For example, roles mapped under Business Information Systems can show different affordability patterns depending on the specific occupation selected:

  • Data Analytics roles may concentrate in high-cost metros but with high salaries
  • Cybersecurity roles may show different geographic demand patterns
  • Systems/IT roles may have more balanced distribution across regions

Similar variations exist across Finance, Marketing, and other majors. This emphasizes that career choice within a major can significantly impact post-graduate financial outcomes, not just the major selection itself.

Actionable Insight: Students should not only choose a major, but also research the geographic distribution and affordability outcomes of specific career paths within that major. This level of specificity enables more targeted career planning.

Finding: The Critical Role of Geographic Visualization

The interactive map plays a vital role in supporting and communicating these findings. By visualizing wage data geographically, patterns across regions become immediately apparent. Users can quickly identify:

  • Regional clusters: Which regions dominate specific career paths
  • Comparative opportunities: How their preferred city compares to alternatives
  • Cost-adjusted rankings: Where nominal vs. adjusted salaries differ most dramatically

The ability to click on individual metropolitan areas and view both raw and adjusted wage data makes affordability differences tangible and easier to interpret. This visualization transforms abstract statistical comparisons into concrete, actionable information.

Finding: Affordability is Multifaceted

A broader finding emerges from the overall analysis: affordability is not a one-dimensional problem, and salary alone is insufficient for location decisions.

The Limitations of Single Metrics: Looking at nominal salary alone can be misleading. Even widely used indicators like rent-to-income ratios do not fully capture the cost of living. Housing is just one component of total living expenses; transportation, food, utilities, and services vary significantly across regions.

The Adjusted Wage Approach: By combining income data with Regional Price Parities, the adjusted wage metric provides a clearer and more balanced perspective. This holistic approach accounts for the full basket of goods and services, not just housing, enabling more informed financial decisions.

While the current model is intentionally simple for accessibility, it effectively highlights meaningful differences between cities and supports more informed decision-making.

Broader Implications & Recommendations

Consider a Wider Range of Cities

The results suggest that many graduates may benefit from considering a wider range of cities beyond the most obvious high-paying markets. Mid-sized metros often offer competitive adjusted salaries with lower living costs and potentially better quality of life.

Prioritize Adjusted Over Nominal Wages

When evaluating job offers or career locations, graduates should prioritize adjusted wage comparisons over nominal salaries. An offer that appears smaller in a low-cost area may actually provide greater purchasing power and financial stability.

Account for Career Path Specificity

Different career paths within a major have different geographic opportunities. Graduates should research where their specific occupation thrives, not just which cities are known for their major.

Explore Trade-offs Proactively

Use tools like TCU Career Compass to explore trade-offs between salary, employment concentration, and cost of living. What trade-offs align with your personal values and financial goals?

Future Analysis Opportunities

While the current analysis provides valuable insights, several additional dimensions could deepen our understanding:

  • Trend Analysis: How have adjusted wages and employment patterns evolved over time? Are certain metros improving or declining?
  • Growth Potential: Which cities and occupations are experiencing the fastest wage and employment growth?
  • Demographic Patterns: How do cost-of-living and wage patterns vary for different demographic groups?
  • Quality-of-Life Metrics: How could we integrate other factors like cultural amenities, climate, and commute times?
  • Student Debt Scenarios: How do different levels of student debt affect location affordability calculations?
