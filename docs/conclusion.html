Conclusion

Reflecting on what this project reveals about affordability, decision-making, and future directions for research.

Evolution & Key Insights

This project initially focused on housing as a single affordability metric. However, housing-specific analysis is complex: rent vs. ownership, number of bedrooms, neighborhood variation within cities, and market timing all affect costs. The project shifted to using Regional Price Parities (RPP), which captures comprehensive cost-of-living including housing, transportation, food, utilities, and services. This holistic approach provides a clearer picture of real purchasing power across regions.

This shift proved essential. By presenting data through an interactive application, the project makes research insights accessible and relevant to college students. Instead of reading academic papers, users can explore their own situations: selecting their major, career path, and target cities to see real wage and affordability data in context.

What emerged from this approach:

💰

Geographic Variation

Nominal salaries can hide dramatic differences in what you can actually afford.

🎯

Career Location Interplay

The best city for a Data Scientist may differ from the best city for a Financial Analyst.

📊

Affordability is Dynamic

What's affordable today changes as labor markets shift, populations relocate, and costs evolve.

🧭

Data Has Limits

While wages and cost-of-living matter, so do community, growth potential, and personal values.

Final Thoughts

The reality is, when you're choosing where to start your career, salary is important. But salary alone doesn't tell the whole story. Use this tool to see the numbers, but don't stop there. Talk to people working in those cities, research companies you actually want to work for, and think about what matters to you beyond the paycheck.

What makes sense for one person might not make sense for you. Some people want to stay close to family. Others are drawn to a specific city's culture or opportunities. This tool is just one piece of the puzzle. You should use it to answer the money question, then make your choice based on everything else that matters to you.

The bottom line is don't just chase the highest salary. Look at the full picture of affordability, opportunity, and what your life actually needs. Your first career move is a big one, so you should make it an informed decision.
