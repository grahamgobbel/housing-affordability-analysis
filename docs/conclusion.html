Conclusion

Reflecting on what this project reveals about affordability, decision-making, and future directions for research.

How The Project Evolved

This project originally set out to answer the question of which major U.S. cities are most and least affordable when comparing housing costs to local income levels, and what those differences might mean for college graduates deciding where to begin their careers. While the original focus was on housing affordability specifically, the project ultimately expanded to incorporate a broader measure of affordability by adjusting wages using Regional Price Parities.

Why This Shift Mattered: Because of this expansion, the project answers the research question in a much more comprehensive way than originally planned. Rather than a narrow focus on rent-to-income ratios, the final tool evaluates overall purchasing power—accounting for housing, transportation, food, utilities, and services across regions. This comprehensive approach provides more actionable insights for recent graduates making location decisions.

Contribution to the Broader Conversation

This project contributes to the broader conversation around housing affordability by shifting the focus from housing costs alone to overall purchasing power. Rather than treating rent or home prices as the primary measure, it demonstrates the importance of evaluating income and cost of living together.

By presenting this through an interactive, data-driven application, the project makes these insights more accessible and relevant to college students and recent graduates. Instead of reading academic papers or news articles about affordability trends, users can explore their own specific situations—selecting their major, career path, and target cities to see real wage and affordability data.

Why This Matters: For a student deciding between staying near family in a high-cost area versus relocating to a more affordable city with better career prospects, this tool provides concrete, personalized information. The research demonstrates that this decision deserves more nuanced analysis than simply chasing the highest salary.

What This Project Reveals About Complexity

Overall, this project shows that affordability is more complex than housing costs alone. By focusing on purchasing power, it provides a clearer and more practical perspective for post-graduate decision-making while also highlighting areas for further exploration.

Key takeaways about complexity:

  • Geographic variation matters more than we might assume. Nominal salaries can mask dramatic differences in real purchasing power.
  • Career path selection interacts with location selection. The "best" city for a Data Scientist may differ from the best city for a Financial Analyst, even within the same major.
  • Affordability is dynamic. What's affordable today may change as labor markets shift, populations relocate, and cost of living evolves.
  • Data-driven doesn't mean complete. While this project incorporates income, employment, and cost-of-living data, other factors—community, career growth potential, personal preferences—remain equally important for location decisions.

Reflections on the Research Process

Building this project reinforced several important lessons about research and decision-making:

Ask Better Questions

The shift from "housing affordability" to "overall affordability" was not a pivot away from the original question—it was a refinement. Better data and a deeper understanding of the problem led to a better question.

Let Data Guide You

The initial assumption that housing costs alone would tell the affordability story proved incomplete. Regional Price Parity data proved more informative and comprehensive, showing the importance of listening to what the data reveals rather than forcing a predetermined narrative.

Make Research Accessible

Academic findings can be powerful, but they're only useful if people engage with them. Building an interactive tool transformed dry statistics into a practical resource that users can explore personally.

Complexity Requires Humility

This project attempts to simplify a complex problem, but that simplification has limits. The best research acknowledges what it cannot answer and points toward future questions rather than overstating its conclusions.

Final Thoughts

For TCU business students and recent graduates, the primary takeaway is straightforward: when making career location decisions, evaluate the whole picture. Compare nominal salaries, but then adjust them for regional cost of living. Explore not just which cities are "hotspots" for your major, but which cities offer the best combination of career opportunity and affordability for your personal situation.

This tool is one input among many. Use it alongside conversations with mentors, research into specific companies and opportunities, and reflection on your own priorities. The goal is not to prescribe a "best" city, but to empower informed decision-making.

Ultimately, affordability—like career success—is personal. What works for one person may not work for another. But by making data more accessible and highlighting the complexity of the affordability problem, this project hopes to support better decisions at a critical juncture: where to live and begin your career.
