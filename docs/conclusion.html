Evolution & Key Insights

This project initially focused on housing as a single affordability metric. However, housing-specific analysis is complex: rent vs. ownership, number of bedrooms, neighborhood variation within cities, and market timing all affect costs. The project shifted to using Regional Price Parities (RPP), which captures comprehensive cost-of-living including housing, transportation, food, utilities, and services. This holistic approach provides a clearer picture of real purchasing power across regions.

This shift proved essential. By presenting data through an interactive application, the project makes research insights accessible and relevant to college students. Instead of reading academic papers, users can explore their own situations: selecting their major, career path, and target cities to see real wage and affordability data in context.

What emerged from this approach:

  • Geographic variation masks real purchasing power. Nominal salaries can hide dramatic differences in what you can actually afford.
  • Career and location decisions interact. The best city for a Data Scientist may differ from the best city for a Financial Analyst, even within the same major.
  • Affordability is dynamic. What's affordable today changes as labor markets shift, populations relocate, and costs evolve.
  • Data-driven decisions have limits. While wages, employment, and cost-of-living matter, so do community, growth potential, and personal preferences.

Reflections on the Research Process

Building this project reinforced several important lessons about research and decision-making:

Ask Better Questions

The shift from housing-only to Regional Price Parities was not a pivot away from the core question. It was a refinement. Housing alone is too narrow (complexity of buy/rent decisions, bedroom counts, location variation). RPP data provided a more complete and actionable answer.

Let Data Guide You

The initial assumption that housing costs alone would tell the affordability story proved incomplete. RPP data—which includes housing plus transportation, food, utilities, and services—proved more informative and comprehensive, showing the importance of listening to what the data reveals rather than forcing a predetermined narrative.

Make Research Accessible

Academic findings can be powerful, but they're only useful if people engage with them. Building an interactive tool transformed dry statistics into a practical resource that users can explore personally.

Complexity Requires Humility

This project attempts to simplify a complex problem, but that simplification has limits. The best research acknowledges what it cannot answer and points toward future questions rather than overstating its conclusions.

Final Thoughts

For TCU business students and recent graduates, the primary takeaway is straightforward: when making career location decisions, evaluate the whole picture. Compare nominal salaries, but then adjust them for regional cost of living. Explore not just which cities are "hotspots" for your major, but which cities offer the best combination of career opportunity and affordability for your personal situation.

This tool is one input among many. Use it alongside conversations with mentors, research into specific companies and opportunities, and reflection on your own priorities. The goal is not to prescribe a "best" city, but to empower informed decision-making.

Ultimately, affordability is like career success: it's personal. What works for one person may not work for another. But by making data more accessible and highlighting the complexity of the affordability problem, this project hopes to support better decisions at a critical juncture: where to live and begin your career.
